Section A
Executive Summary
Decision-grade assessment for the Prime Minister, Finance Minister, SBP Governor, and NSC. Prepared 7 March 2026.

Situation. On 28 February 2026, the United States and Israel conducted joint strikes on Iran, including the killing of Iran's supreme leader. Iran retaliated with missile and drone attacks. The IRGC warned against vessel passage through the Strait of Hormuz. Tanker traffic dropped approximately 70% within 48 hours. Brent crude breached $90/bbl for the first time in two years. Major shipping lines (Maersk, MSC, Hapag-Lloyd, CMA CGM) have suspended Hormuz transit.

Pakistan Exposure. Pakistan imports ~300,000 bbl/day of crude, with over 94% sourced from Gulf states (UAE + Saudi Arabia) via the Strait of Hormuz. Pakistan also receives 9 LNG cargoes/month from Qatar under long-term contracts priced at 10.2–13.37% of Brent. The oil import bill was $10.71 billion in the first 8 months of FY25. Total energy imports account for ~30% of the import bill.

Central Assessment. Under the current disruption (Scenario 1–2 range), Pakistan faces an additional $2–5 billion annual import bill, CPI inflation rising from 7% to 9–12% within 3 months, rupee depreciation of 3–8%, and a current account deterioration of $1.5–4 billion. If closure extends beyond 1 month (Scenario 3–4), inflation could spike to 14–20%, FX reserves could fall below $12 billion, and Pakistan's IMF program could face stress testing.

Critical Window. The next 2–4 weeks are decisive. Policy actions taken within 72 hours on strategic reserves, FX intervention, and fiscal buffers will determine whether Pakistan manages an orderly adjustment or faces a 2022-style external account crisis.

Section C
Inflation Transmission Map — Pakistan-Specific
Seven channels through which Hormuz disruption reaches Pakistani consumers and the fiscal accounts. First-round effects hit within days; second-round effects build over 1–6 months.
HORMUZ DISRUPTION — TRIGGER EVENT
▼ ▼ ▼ ▼ ▼ ▼ ▼
Channel 1
Crude Oil & Petroleum
Direct. 300K bbl/day imports, 94% from Gulf via Hormuz. Brent spike → OGRA fortnightly price revision → petrol/diesel +Rs 15–80/L depending on scenario. Pass-through: 2–15 days.
Channel 2
LNG & Power Generation
Direct. 9 Qatar LNG cargoes/month via Hormuz. Prices at 10.2–13.37% of Brent. At $130 Brent → LNG contract price doubles. RLNG-based electricity cost surges → FCA adjustment. Pass-through: 1–4 weeks.
Channel 3
Freight & Insurance
Direct. War risk insurance from 0.05% to 1–3% of cargo value. Container rerouting via Cape of Good Hope adds 10–14 days. All imports affected, not just energy. Pass-through: 1–6 weeks.
▼ ▼ ▼
Channel 4
Exchange Rate
Amplifier. Higher import bill → FX demand surge → PKR depreciation → all imports become more expensive in rupee terms. FX reserves drawdown weakens SBP intervention capacity. 10% PKR fall adds ~0.8–1.2 ppt to CPI.
Channel 5
Fuel & Utility Pricing
First-round CPI. Petrol/diesel → transport CPI (7.2% weight). Electricity/gas → housing CPI (23.6% weight). Combined energy weight in CPI basket: ~15–18%. Direct CPI pass-through within 1 month.
▼ ▼
Channel 6
Food & Fertilizer
Second-round. Transport cost → food distribution cost (+3–8%). DAP fertilizer (imported) price surge → Rabi/Kharif input costs rise → food prices with 2–6 month lag. Pakistan's food CPI weight: ~35%. Wheat, edible oil particularly vulnerable.
Channel 7
Expectations & Second-Round
Second-round. Wage-price spiral if expectations de-anchor. Core inflation rises as businesses pass through costs. SBP credibility tested. Risk of 2022 repeat: inflation expectations jumped from 9% to 25% in 3 months. Critical to manage narrative early.

Section F
Policy Response Menu
Three-tier response framework for coordinated government action. Each tier activates as crisis indicators cross defined thresholds.
Tier 1 — Immediate Actions (Within 72 Hours)
ACTIVATE WHEN: Brent > $90 sustained or Hormuz transits < 50% of normal
  • Activate National Energy Crisis Cell under PM Secretariat. Daily briefings with SBP, Petroleum Division, MoF, OGRA, Power Division.
  • Assess strategic petroleum reserve status (currently ~20 days cover). Instruct PSO/PARCO to maximize storage utilization.
  • SBP FX intervention protocol: Authorize measured FX sales to prevent disorderly PKR depreciation. Signal commitment to orderly market.
  • Engage Saudi Arabia and UAE bilaterally for emergency crude supply via Red Sea ports (Yanbu, Jeddah) bypassing Hormuz. Saudi ARAMCO has Red Sea export capacity of 5M bbl/day.
  • Contact IMF mission chief to brief on force majeure risks. Seek flexibility on fiscal targets if oil exceeds $110.
  • Defer OGRA fuel price hike by 48–72 hours to prevent panic buying. Announce measured, not full, pass-through.
  • Issue NDMA advisory on fuel conservation measures. Prepare contingency for transport sector disruption.
  • Instruct NEPRA to model FCA scenarios and prepare communication strategy for electricity tariff adjustments.
Tier 2 — Near-Term Actions (Within 2 Weeks)
ACTIVATE WHEN: Brent > $110 or Hormuz closure confirmed > 48 hours
  • Negotiate emergency oil swap with Saudi Arabia: crude via Red Sea tanker to Karachi on deferred payment (G2G). Pakistan has precedent from 2023 Saudi facility.
  • Activate partial fuel subsidy for transport sector only (Rs 15–25/L cap on diesel). Estimated cost Rs 8–12B/month.
  • SBP to halt rate-cut expectations: Issue emergency forward guidance. If Brent >$120, prepare 50–100bps emergency hike.
  • Accelerate non-Gulf crude sourcing: Instruct PARCO/PRL to tender for Russian Urals, West African, or US crude via Cape route (longer lead time: 4–6 weeks).
  • Release fertilizer buffer stocks through NFDC. Cap DAP prices at Rs 16,000/bag through subsidy. Rabi crop protection is critical.
  • Ministry of Commerce: Fast-track import substitution for edible oil (palm oil from Malaysia/Indonesia — not Hormuz dependent).
  • Engage China: Request CPEC energy facility acceleration. Explore CNY-denominated crude purchase to reduce FX pressure.
  • Communication strategy: PM/FM joint presser projecting calm. Publish fuel reserve data to prevent hoarding.
Tier 3 — Contingency Actions (1–3 Months)
ACTIVATE WHEN: Brent > $130 sustained or full closure exceeds 2 weeks
  • Emergency supplementary budget: Additional Rs 300–600B allocation for energy subsidies. Cut non-essential PSDP by 30%.
  • SBP emergency measures: Temporary import compression — prioritize energy, food, medicines. Restrict non-essential imports via LCs.
  • Seek emergency bilateral support: Saudi/UAE deposits ($2–3B each, as in 2022). China SAFE swap line activation ($4.5B). IMF Rapid Financing Instrument if needed.
  • Fuel rationing framework: If supply disruption exceeds 40%, implement tiered rationing via Ehsaas program infrastructure. Priority: agriculture, essential transport, hospitals.
  • Demand-side measures: Government fleet restrictions, work-from-home directives, reduced commercial hours. Estimated 10–15% demand reduction.
  • Maximize domestic gas: Divert all RLNG to power sector. Prioritize pipeline gas for fertilizer plants. Instruction to SSGC/SNGPL.
  • IMF program renegotiation: If fiscal targets breached, seek waiver or program augmentation. Historical precedent exists (2022 floods).
  • Food security: Release wheat from PASSCO strategic reserves. Monitor atta/sugar/ghee prices at tehsil level. Deploy price control magistrates.

Key Assumptions
Analytical Framework — Assumptions, Confidence & Red Team

Key Assumptions Used

  • Oil price baseline: Pre-crisis Brent at ~$75/bbl. Current $85–93 already reflects risk premium. Assumptions for each scenario layer additional price impact on top of this.
  • Gulf dependency: 94%+ of Pakistan crude from UAE/Saudi Arabia transits Hormuz (based on MEES 2019 data, updated with S&P Global 2024 analysis). Proxy used as GoP does not publish real-time route-level data.
  • CPI weights: Based on PBS CPI basket (2015-16 base). Energy direct weight ~15–18%; food weight ~35%. Exchange rate pass-through coefficient estimated at 0.08–0.12 (i.e., 10% PKR depreciation → 0.8–1.2 ppt CPI impact, based on SBP working papers).
  • Strategic reserves: Assumed at ~20 days of cover (industry estimate; exact GoP figures classified). Refinery crude stocks estimated at 7–10 days additional cover.
  • Alternative supply routes: Saudi Yanbu/Red Sea crude can partially substitute within 2–4 weeks. Russian Urals or West African crude requires 4–8 weeks to establish.
  • IMF program: Assumed Pakistan remains in active EFF program with fiscal deficit targets of ~6–6.5% of GDP. Breach triggers waiver negotiations, not automatic termination.
  • Remittances: Gulf worker remittances ($25B+ annually from GCC) assumed to remain stable under S0–S2. Under S3–S4, 5–15% disruption possible from regional instability.

Upside Risks (Things Could Be Better)

Upside Scenarios

Rapid de-escalation: Ceasefire within 1–2 weeks through GCC/Turkey mediation. Brent retraces to $80–85. Pakistan exposure limited to S0.

US Navy convoy success: Trump's proposed tanker escort scheme works. Insurance rates normalize. Transit resumes at 50–70% capacity within weeks.

Saudi Red Sea pivot: Saudi ARAMCO redirects 1–2M bbl/day through Yanbu pipeline to Red Sea. Pakistan secures G2G supply at competitive pricing.

Global SPR release: IEA coordinates strategic reserve release (200M+ barrels available). Brent capped below $120 even under S2.

Downside Risks (Things Could Be Worse)

Downside Scenarios

War spreads to GCC: If Iran attacks Saudi/UAE oil infrastructure (Abqaiq 2.0 scenario), even Red Sea alternatives are disrupted. Brent could exceed $200.

Mining of Hormuz: Iran deploys naval mines in the strait. Even after ceasefire, mine-clearing takes 2–6 months. Structural disruption to supply.

Pakistan-specific FX crisis: If reserves drop below $10B, parallel market emerges, PKR falls 25%+. IMF program suspension. This would amplify all scenario impacts by 50–100%.

Gulf remittance disruption: If conflict extends to UAE/Saudi, 2M+ Pakistani workers face displacement. Remittance inflows ($25B+ from GCC) disrupted. Current account collapses.

Domestic political crisis: Fuel/food protests trigger political instability. Government falls or military intervention. Policy paralysis amplifies economic damage.

Red Team Assessment — Where This Analysis Could Be Wrong

Challenge 1: Gulf Dependency May Be Higher Than Stated

We use 94% as the Gulf crude dependency figure (MEES 2019). Actual 2025–26 dependency may be closer to 97–99% given that recent US crude trial cargoes were one-off and Russia supply never materialized at scale. If true, our S2–S4 impact estimates are conservative.

Challenge 2: Strategic Reserves Are Likely Lower Than Assumed

We assume ~20 days of petroleum reserves. Industry sources suggest operational stocks may be as low as 12–15 days during low-demand periods. If a supply crunch hits during summer (peak demand), reserves could deplete in under 10 days. Government has no formal SPR separate from commercial stocks.

Challenge 3: FX Pass-Through Could Be Non-Linear

Our models assume 10% PKR depreciation → ~1 ppt CPI. But Pakistan's 2022 experience showed pass-through accelerates at stress points: from linear below 15% depreciation to exponential above 20%. A confidence break in the rupee changes the math entirely.

Challenge 4: Second-Round Effects Are Underestimated in Short Horizons

Our 1-month CPI estimates capture mainly first-round energy price effects. But Pakistan's informal economy transmits price signals faster than formal models assume. Atta prices can jump 15% within 72 hours of a fuel price hike as transporters immediately raise rates. Our 1-month numbers may be too conservative.

Challenge 5: Policy Response Assumes Rational, Coordinated Action

The policy menu assumes government agencies coordinate effectively and make timely decisions. Pakistan's track record on inter-ministry coordination during crises (2022 floods, 2023 FX crisis) suggests implementation lag of 2–4 weeks. By then, markets may have already priced in worst-case scenarios.

Challenge 6: This Analysis Cannot Model Black Swans

A direct attack on Gwadar port, a nuclear dimension to the Iran conflict, or a simultaneous India-Pakistan border incident would create compounding risks that no linear scenario model captures. These are tail risks but not zero-probability in the current environment.
